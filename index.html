Exam Technique

The difference between a B and an A is almost never knowledge — it's applying it correctly under pressure.

AQA Command Words — What They Actually Mean
The 6-Mark Question Framework
1
State the governing physics principle
Name the law or conservation rule. "By Faraday's law of electromagnetic induction..." or "Using conservation of momentum...". Examiners look for this first.
2
Define the key quantities you'll use
Don't assume the examiner infers understanding. Define flux linkage, SHM restoring force, binding energy — whatever is central to the physics.
3
Describe the mechanism step-by-step using causal language
Use "this causes...", "therefore...", "which results in...". Each mark point is a logical step, not a list of facts.
4
Include relevant equations or proportionalities
Even in "explain" questions, writing "F ∝ 1/r², so doubling distance reduces force by factor 4" secures marks. Show mathematical understanding.
5
Conclude with a direct answer to the question asked
The final mark often goes for a clear concluding statement. "Therefore the current increases." "Hence the star is a red giant." Don't leave it implicit.
Common Errors That Cost Marks
✗Using g = 10 — AQA uses 9.81 m s⁻²
✗Rounding intermediate values — only round the final answer
✗Writing "energy is lost" — say WHERE (heat, sound, etc.)
✗Forgetting units on numerical answers
✗Using Celsius instead of Kelvin in gas / thermal equations
✗Using r from the surface not the centre in field equations
✗Confusing weight (N) with mass (kg) in F = ma
✗Forgetting to square v in E = ½mv² and F = mv²/r
✗Adding absolute uncertainties for × ÷ instead of percentages
✗Confusing EMF ε with terminal voltage V (they differ by Ir)
